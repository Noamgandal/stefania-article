ABSTRACT

This paper examines Taddeo di Bartolo's depiction of Hell in the Collegiata di Santa Maria Assunta, the mother church of San Gimignano. In a striking departure from similar scenes of the period, the fresco, painted in the early fifteenth century, emphasizes the obesity of the sinners—suggesting a deliberate visual critique. I argue that the fresco functioned as a public denunciation of the Dominican order, which sought to control religious institutions and appropriate the ancient church of San Lorenzo in Ponte after being forcibly relocated nearby. Parishioners, aware of the conflict and the friars' opulent lifestyle, likely associated the grotesquely corpulent sinners—particularly the bloated friar at the gluttons' table—with the Dominicans. Sophisticated viewers would have linked this imagery to Aquinas' condemnation of gluttony and Dante's Inferno (Canto XI), where the Dominicans were explicitly criticized as ghiotti, symbolizing moral and spiritual decay. By situating the fresco within contemporary religious politics and evolving conceptions of corpulence as a moral failing, this study offers a new insight into the intersection of artistic representation, theological discourse and literary tradition in early fifteenth-century Italy.

1 Introduction

Upon ascending an unassuming high staircase on the west side of Piazza del Duomo in San Gimignano, visitors are in awe as they enter the church of the Collegiata di Santa Maria Assunta. The astounding walls, adorned with medieval frescoes, leave a profound impression. These largely intact frescoes were painted over a period of seventy years in the fourteenth century and the beginning of the fifteenth century. The church was known to the town's medieval inhabitants simply as the Pieve and is now referred to as the Collegiata di San Gimignano.

The south aisle features Bartolo di Fredi's Old Testament Cycle, and the north aisle showcases scenes from the New Testament. The controfacciata and adjacent walls of the nave display the Last Judgement, masterfully painted by Taddeo di Bartolo (c. 1362–1422).1 In particular, the fresco on the south wall of the central nave, positioned above the first arch of the nave arcade, vividly portrays Hell, where Taddeo explicated abstract vices by depicting specific transgressions and their corresponding punishments.2

The fresco depicts numerous sinners undergoing demonic torment, a conventional iconographic element in late medieval representations of Hell. What distinguishes this work from other Italian examples of the period is the deliberate emphasis on the corpulence of the damned. In contrast to earlier and later representations, Taddeo depicts the sinners with conspicuously obese bodies featuring distended abdomens, rounded haunches and heavily built limbs. While earlier scholarship has acknowledged the physical corpulence of these sinners, it has not explored the historical and literary contexts that might account for this portrayal.3

This paper explores Taddeo di Bartolo's portrayal of obesity in his Hell fresco, arguing that the work functioned not solely as a religious message but also as a political statement. Taddeo created this work during a conflict between the canons of the Collegiata and the Dominican Order over control of the city's religious institutions. Consequently, in response to the Dominicans' actions against their church, the patrons of the Collegiata sought a public denouncement. My thesis proposes the fresco as a visual manifestation of the power struggle and rivalry between the Collegiata and the Dominican Order.

I begin by examining the historical context through a map of San Gimignano to trace the Dominican convent's relocation within the city. This relocation sheds light on the underlying reason behind the friction: the Dominicans' unwavering determination to take over the church of San Lorenzo in Ponte, which was affiliated with the Collegiata. I present evidence demonstrating that patterns of misbehaviour and indiscipline were common among Dominican Orders in this period. Interestingly, Dante's La Divina Commedia echoes and reinforces this narrative by admonishing the moral decline of the Dominicans. Dante, who visited San Gimignano, uses their excessive indulgence in food as a symbolic representation of moral failing. The visual motif of corpulence as moral decline was part of an ongoing evolution of symbolic representations in fourteenth-century art, as exemplified by Dante's contemporary Giotto in the Scrovegni Chapel and in a miniature from the Cocharelli Codex.

In the second part of this study, my focus shifts to Taddeo's depiction of sinners in Hell and other related visual imagery. This analysis first establishes the distinctive representation of corpulent sinners in Taddeo's fresco. I then demonstrate that monumental visual art in this period frequently conveyed political messages and examine through physiognomic science how bodily and facial features served as visual markers of moral failing. Together, this evidence supports the conclusion that Taddeo's fresco functions as a political denunciation of the mendicant orders' attempt to seize control of an institution under the Collegiata's jurisdiction.

2 San Gimignano, Dante Alighieri and the Dominicans

San Gimignano, a picturesque medieval hill town in the Val d'Elsa, province of Siena, is renowned for its iconic defensive towers.4 Positioned on the historic Via Francigena, which connected Western Europe to Rome along the route between Lucca and Siena, the town held significant strategic importance in the region.5 During its golden age, at the turn of the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, San Gimignano enjoyed modest military and political prominence.6 This prominence was evident in May 1300, when Dante Alighieri was sent to the town as an ambassador of the Guelph League in Tuscany. His mission was to persuade the local officials to provide added troops in support of Pope Boniface VIII's campaign in the region.7 Dante was received by the Podestà Mino di Simone Tolomei—brother of the poet Meo, with whom Dante had exchanged sonnets.8 Given Dante's involvement in political affairs and beyond, it is reasonable to assume that he was both a familiar and esteemed figure in San Gimignano.

In the thirteenth century, the Collegiata served as the ‘Mother Church’ for several rural parishes affiliated with it.9 Among these was the Church of San Lorenzo in Ponte, situated about 250 m from the Collegiata on Via del Castello. The Dominicans, who settled in the Valdelsa in 1318, were granted the rights to build a convent on Montestaffoli, a hill located behind the Collegiata, challenging its authority.10

The Dominicans had only a brief presence at Montestaffoli. Soon after, the devastating Black Death of 1348 struck leading to a sharp population decline and political instability in San Gimignano. These crises forced the town to submit to Florence and become part of its Distretto in 1353.11

Shortly after the Florentines gained control over the land of San Gimignano, they opted to replace the strategically located Dominican institution with a fortress called Rocca di Montestaffoli. This decision was made to strengthen the Florentine's hold over the most powerful residents of the area.12 In return, the Dominicans were granted monetary compensation and a new location for their convent at the end of Via del Castello. The Dominican Order assumed control of this part of the city and reorganized its layout: they acquired the Church of S. Stefano in Canova and closed two streets, one of which ran through one of their gardens, installing gates at either end to be opened only in times of war.13

They also obtained nearby buildings from inherited estates and started construction on the sumptuous Church of the Santissima Annunziata, thus establishing a new religious presence just meters away from San Lorenzo in Ponte, which was affiliated with the Collegiata.14 Their goal was to strengthen their influence and create new lay congregations to support their growing wealth.

On the right, we see the new location of the convent, which is situated right next to the church of San Lorenzo in Ponte (Figure 1).

The course of these events resulted in tensions between the clerics of the Collegiata and the Dominican Order in its new location. The Dominican friars sought to strengthen their spiritual jurisdiction, at the expense of the Collegiata, by requesting the Holy See to assume control of the nearby church of San Lorenzo in Ponte, which was in close proximity to their new convent. This request led to a decades-long dispute over the church's control. In the end, despite opposition from the city leaders, Pope Eugenius IV granted the Dominican's request in 1435.15

Taddeo di Bartolo's Inferno was painted in the Collegiata in the second decade of the fifteenth century,16 in the timeframe between 1353 when the Dominicans moved to the new location and the Pope's injunction—in favour of the Dominincans—in 1435.

By the fourteenth century the Dominican Order was firmly established as a major institutional force within the Roman Catholic Church, but it was also facing challenges and scrutiny for corruption and moral failures.17 In his examination of primary sources regarding early Dominican discipline, Guy Geltner reports over 100 incidents of violence, implicating hundreds of friars, during the period 1250–1400.18

Contemporary figures Giovanni Villani (c. 1280–1348) and Giovanni Dominici (1356–1419) criticized the Dominicans for corruption and excess—Villani for their abuse of authority and greed, and Dominici for their lavish building projects, social luxury, neglect of communal life and misuse of funds—calling for a return to the order's original ideals of simplicity and apostolic poverty.19

Pecori records a case from San Gimignano when in 1424, a ten-year-old child from a family of parishioners of the Collegiata was taken to a Dominican convent in Siena. The incident provoked public complaints about the friars' disorderly conduct, poor governance and disregard for communal authority, which were brought before the public council in San Gimignano.20 ‘Rescue missions’ under similar circumstances in Florence and Bologna are documented in Geltner's book.21 Although I have not found additional specific references to problems with the Dominican Order in San Gimignano, there is also no indication that the town's experience differed from that of other communities in the region.

Moreover, Dante expressed his concern about the declining moral standards of the Dominican Order and made harsh comments on the challenges facing the Dominicans in Paradiso, Canto XI:

Ma 'l suo pecuglio di nova vivanda

è fatto ghiotto, sì ch'esser non puote

che per diversi salti non si spanda;

e quanto le sue pecore remote

e vagabunde più da esso vanno,

più tornano a l'ovil di latte vòte.

Ben son di quelle che temono 'l danno

e stringonsi al pastor; ma son sì poche,

che le cappe fornisce poco panno22

Dante features the Dominican friars as ghiotti, gluttonous, which symbolizes materialism and deviation from the morals of the church. He uses a flock wandering away from their shepherd, St Dominic, as a metaphor.23 The excessive indulgence in food and drink by clergymen and religious figures was a recurring theme in that period. For example, Dominican Remigio dei Girolami (c. 1235–1310) in De Bono Pacis insinuated similar behaviour. In a sermon before friars in Florence he listed the accusations of laymen toward the clergy as ‘fornicators, gluttons, rapacious, lazy’.24

There is evidence that Dante's Divina Commedia reached San Gimignano very early, likely during Dante's lifetime. As early as 1317, a notary from San Gimignano copied verses from the third canto of Inferno onto the cover of a register of criminal acts, now preserved in the State Archives of Bologna.25 Also a small fragment of Dante's Paradiso (Par. xiv 100–xxiv 60), dating from the third or last quarter of the fourteenth century and once belonging to the Useppi family of San Gimignano, was long considered lost until it was anonymously returned in 2021.26 This strongly suggests that some individuals in San Gimignano were already familiar with Dante's views on the Dominicans. This early awareness of Dante's writings could have contributed to the spread of sentiment about the Dominicans' controversial actions.

Taken together, the discussion in this section suggests that the Convent of San Domenico in San Gimignano was competing with the Collegiata. The Dominicans' presumptuous behaviour in the town was consistent with the moral crisis the order had faced since the late thirteenth century. This conduct was even admonished by Dante, whose Commedia was known in San Gimignano as early as 1317; he used the metaphor of gluttony to express his disapproval of the friars' behaviour.

In the following sections the focus shifts to Taddeo's fresco, through a comparative analysis with other artworks of a similar theme considering the evolution of corpulence as a moral signifier.

3 Corpulence as a Moral Signifier

Between the 13th and early 15th centuries, Italian visual culture developed a sophisticated vocabulary for depicting corpulence that integrated theological categories of sin, humoral medical theory and physiognomic science. Dominican theologian, Thomas Aquinas (c. 1265–1274), systematized the doctrine of gluttony27 and Albertus Magnus (c. 1200–1280) legitimated physiognomic theory within Christian natural philosophy in De Animalibus, one of the earliest medieval commentaries on ancient Greek and Arabic physiognomic ideas.28 Dominican writer Giovanni da San Gimignano (c. 1260—after 1333) argued, in the early thirteenth century, that great fatness not only ‘engenders the worst maladies’ but ‘impedes the activity of the soul’ and is thus an ‘obstacle to sense and intelligence’.29

The conclusive connection between excessive eating, gluttony and fatness emerged in the late thirteenth and early fourteenth centuries. This shift occurred within a complex landscape where multiple meanings of corpulence coexisted: in political contexts, the ‘big person’ signified vigour and power; in contexts of scarcity, corpulence marked privileged access to food; from a medical perspective, a large physique could be explained by an individual's humoral complexion or the physical demands of their profession. The mendicant orders introduced a transformative framework that revalued bodily size. Emphasizing fasting, poverty and asceticism, mendicant preachers in urban centres established thinness as a marker of spiritual authority, creating a visual rhetoric of bodily contrast between disciplined sanctity and worldly excess.30

This new moral framework demanded systematic visual representations for penitential practices. Artists responded by developing physiognomically precise depictions of sin that distinguished culpable gluttony from innocent constitutional fullness through specific markers: distended bellies, particular facial features, posture and colouring. These visual vocabularies enabled corpulence to function as a legible moral signifier within the urban penitential culture shaped by mendicant preaching. I will provide physiognomic analysis of illustrations later in this paper.

A striking example of a painting which exemplifies very clearly the concept of corpulence as a moral signifier is found in Cocharelli Codex, in Treatise on the Vices (late 14th C), BL Add MS 27695, f.14r (Figure 3). This illuminated miniature depicts the vice of gluttony through a two-tiered composition showing a tavern scene above and a wine cellar below. The upper scene features several drinkers seated at a yellow table. Heavy physique dominates the composition. The most striking feature is the pronounced corpulence of the central figures, particularly the man in a grey gown who is distinctly rounded. The figure in a blue gown beside him similarly displays a substantial, rounded body. Their flushed, reddened complexions signal drunkenness. Both hold large glasses or goblets raised to their lips, drinking in excess, their postures lacking composure. In contrast, the slimmer figure in a pink gown, on the left, sits upright and composed, his skin light and smooth, holding a small glass and drinking in moderation, providing both physical and behavioural contrast to the corpulent gluttons. Below, a cellarer with a notably rounded belly works among large wine barrels, including a prominently displayed striped barrel that emphasizes the abundant supply fuelling the excess above. The deliberate rendering of corpulence functions as a visual moral code—the swollen bodies of the drinkers and the cellarer serve as physical manifestations of their vice, making gluttony legible through flesh. This aligns with medieval physiognomic traditions where bodily form revealed inner character, the corpulent body becoming a visible sign of sin, excess and lack of temperance.

Giotto's Wedding at Cana (1304–6) in the Scrovegni Chapel offers one of the earliest European examples of corpulence as a negative moral signifier, consistent with medieval physiognomic traditions like the Secretum Secretorum and early precursor of Albertus' work.31 The master of the feast—the fat wine steward—his swollen face and reddish, upturned nose correspond to physiognomic aphorisms for a deceptive character.32 This reading is reinforced by the presence of the allegorical figure of Stultitia (Folly) in the same chapel, who is depicted with a bloated abdomen.33 The steward's substantial build contrasts with the leaner figures of Christ and the disciples, positioning him within the worldly realm of appetite and sensual pleasure as opposed to the spiritual realm of Christ's miracle. This early work demonstrates how Giotto utilized physiognomic conventions from recently translated texts to encode moral judgement through specific bodily markers (Figure 4).

When comparing the facial features of the steward and his helper to the noses of the other figures, it is important to notice that the steward has a swollen face and a reddish nose which is slanted upwards, while Christ's disciple's slender faces have straight noses, a contrast which is a legible sign of spiritual failing (Figure 5).

4 Taddeo di Bartolo and Sinners in Hell

Little information is available about the life and family of Taddeo di Bartolo (c.1362), before he is mentioned in certain documents dating back to 1382 and 1386.34 Around 1390, shortly after completing his apprenticeship, the painter began travelling for commissions, a pattern that would persist for the next decade.35 Subsequently, in the early decades of the fifteenth century, Taddeo returned to Siena and rose to become the most esteemed painter in the city. His reputation also extended to neighbouring towns, where he continued to receive prestigious commissions. Toward the end of his career, around 1420, Taddeo entered a partnership with the younger Gregorio di Cecco. However, by 1422, Taddeo had passed away.36

In the upcoming sections, I delve into Taddeo's portrayal of Hell painted in the second decade of the fifteenth century (Figure 6).37

The fresco is positioned above the first arch of the nave arcade, allowing parishioners to easily note the exaggerated corpulence of the sinners compared to the slender figures of Christ and his companions on the two adjacent walls, completing the scenes of the Last Judgement. Additionally, observers could glimpse through the arch and observe the more proportionate images of Adam and Eve—the primal sinners—depicted in the Old Testament cycle by Bartolo di Fredi, which is aligned just behind this fresco.38 These striking visual contrasts seem intentionally crafted to help parishioners decode the moral lessons linking different areas of the church via the frescoes.39

Taddeo's depiction of Hell is structured into three horizontal registers. The top one, in the lunette above the arch, depicts two zones of Hell, flanking Lucifer: Pride (superbia) on the right of Lucifer and Envy (invidia) on his left. The middle register is divided into three compartments, separated by twisted tree trunks and roots and labelled, from left to right, Gluttony (gula), Avarice (avaritia) and Lust (luxuria). The lower register around the arch is divided into two compartments also separated by a tree trunk. Though the labels on the two lowest compartments are now illegible, a comparison with the Hell scene in the Pisan Camposanto indicates that Sloth (acedia) is on the left, and Wrath (ira) is on the right.40

Among the sinners in the upper lunette, some are clearly labelled historical and biblical figures, such as Cain, Nero and Herod, while most remain unnamed (Figure 6).41

In the avarice compartment (Figure 7, left), a usurer (Usuraio) lies on his back with a dramatically bloated, distended abdomen while a demon crouches on his chest and expels golden coins into his mouth.42 His pronounced corpulence contrasts sharply with the skeletal figure hanging above him, whose emaciated form suggests he consumed his own substance for worldly goods. The demon handling this skeletal figure displays a broad, upturned nose, a physiognomic feature associated with base and bestial characteristics. In the lust compartment (Figure 7 right), a standing figure displays similar corporeal excess, with a notably rounded, protruding belly as demons torment him. Below lies a prone figure identified as a sodomite (Sotomutto). The deliberate depiction of bloated bodies in both compartments creates a visual correlation between different forms of excessive appetite—material greed and carnal lust—manifested through corpulent physiques. Throughout both scenes, flames and demonic torments intensify the punishments.

According to Mann, churchgoers would have readily recognized the behaviours of Taddeo di Bartolo's sinners, whose punishments are depicted with striking immediacy and tangible detail. He argues that parishioners could easily associate these familiar figures, shown under Lucifer's dominion, with the reality of eternal damnation.43 Viewers, in turn, might have imaginatively connected the anonymous sinners to contemporary individuals, potentially identifying specific wrongdoers. Taddeo, I suggest, employs what Mary Carruthers terms ‘associative chains’—a mnemonic strategy that guides viewers from visual stimulus to moral recollection.44 Building on this technique, I contend that Taddeo adapts the mechanism of associative memory to insinuate specific moral failings and, perhaps, to allude to particular offenders—notably the Dominicans. This method of conveying layered messages was not new in the Collegiata. Mann argues that the New Testament cycle by Bartolo di Fredi, commissioned by the commune's most powerful guild, similarly responded to the increasing competition with mendicant orders in the town.45

The following analysis presents evidence supporting the claim that Taddeo's Hell fresco features notably fleshier sinners than those typically found in fourteenth-century Italian painting. Furthermore, I will investigate how the established practice of integrating political commentary into religious frescoes likely shaped Taddeo's work. To support my thesis, I examine depictions of sinners in Hell: The Baptistery of San Giovanni, Florence (Coppo di Marcovaldo, end of the thirteenth century), Scrovegni Chapel, Padua (Giotto di Bondone, beginning of the fourteenth century), Camposanto, Pisa (Buonamico Buffalmaco, fourth decade of the fourteenth century46).47 These examples were chosen as they preceded Taddeo's fresco. More significantly, according to Solberg, Taddeo travelled to Pisa, Padua and Florence around 1390, where he likely viewed these portrayals of Hell, with the Pisan frescoes serving as his primary inspiration for Hell in the Collegiata.48 Thus, Taddeo was familiar with the bodily proportions of the damned in his artistic models. Yet he deliberately chose to paint considerably more corpulent sinners.

The mosaics in the octagonal cupola of the Baptistery of San Giovanni predate most Florentine art and were among the most prominent works of their time. Both Giotto and Dante were familiar with them in their early years. In the depiction of Hell, sinners appear emaciated, their bodies reduced to skeletal forms. Notably, some unsympathetic political figures of the time are depicted in this mosaic. For example, Brendan Cassidy writes that Pope Nicholas V has been identified in the Inferno in the Florentine Baptistery (Figure 8).49

Significantly, the sinners depicted in Giotto's fresco in the Scrovegni Chapel are not as emaciated as those found in the Florence Baptistery, yet even the more corpulent figures are not portrayed with the extreme corpulence seen in Taddeo di Bartolo's vision of Hell. The chapel holds a powerful political message, with Scrovegni himself portrayed in the foreground of the painting. Dante's own condemnation of Reginaldo's misdeeds is reinforced by the extraordinary emphasis on the punishment of usury in the lower area of Giotto's fresco.50 Notably, the painter also included tonsured clerics among the damned souls in his portrayal of Hell. Cassidy notes that Augustinian friars, whose church stood nearby, resented the chapel for drawing parishioners and for the papal indulgences attached to it. The hostility persisted for years, and the inclusion of clerics in Hell—some recognizable by their habits—may reflect these tensions.51 In summary, Giotto's fresco employs two strategies that Taddeo would later adopt: it uses Dante to corroborate condemnation of an unpopular figure, and it subtly depicts Augustinian friars in Hell reflecting power rivalries between the mendicant order and the chapel (Figure 9).

The cycle of frescoes, on the walls of the monumental Camposanto Cemetery of Pisa, were Taddeo's most likely model for his work. Baschet points out that what set the Pisan Hell apart from earlier iconographic traditions of Hell, was its bold focus on gruesome scenes of torture, drawing intense emotional reactions from viewers.52 Also in San Gimignano the focus on corporal tortures is exposed to the extreme. Unlike Taddeo's, in Buffalmacco's composition, the sinners display a range of body types, with some displaying moderate corpulence while the majority possess normative bodily proportions (Figure 10).

5 Taddeo's Depiction of Gluttony

In Taddeo's Gluttony, artistically borrowed from the Pisan Camposanto (Figure 11 left), the sinners are gathered around a round table laden with tempting food, yet they are tormented by demons who forever prevent them from eating.53 A similar theme was also portrayed by Beato Angelico's Last Judgement a few decades after Taddeo's fresco (Figure 11 right).

However, when comparing Taddeo's gluttons to those depicted by Buffalmacco and Beato Angelico, we clearly observe that Taddeo's sinners are noticeably fatter (Figure 12).

In the forefront of the gluttons' table, a contorted man struggles to shield himself from a serpent's bite while adopting a pose that exposes his lower body and suggests profound humiliation. Next to him, Taddeo distinctly emphasizes the presence of a tonsured friar. This conspicuous friar, excessively obese, finds himself coerced by a beaked demon, who forcefully twists his arm. Mann points out that the demon next to him employs a familiar gesture of silent insult, intensifying the friar's ridicule and disdain (see red circle).54 The friar's physique is characterized by a robust frame and a pronounced, rounded abdomen, displaying distinct folds of fat along the sides. His neck appears thick and fleshy, merging into his rounded shoulders. His posterior is engulfed in flames. His facial features include full, fleshy lips, a broad face and a double chin. He has a flattened, upturned nose, similar to that of the demon handling the skeletal figure described earlier. McRae's study on Albertus Magnus identifies several physiognomic traits that directly apply to Taddeo's glutton friar. A protruding belly signals wickedness, malice and deceit.55 Thick, turned-back lips indicate stupidity and voracity.56 Furthermore, a dense redness in complexion is said to reveal a deceitful nature and large, protruding ear lobes denote dullness and imprudence.57 Red hair that fades to whiteness indicates lack of understanding and an evil way of life—a colour shared by all the sinners except the friar. Conversely, black hair is recognized as one of the physiognomic characteristics of the perfect companion for kings.58 As black hair is absent from all other sinners in the fresco, this distinction may signify a deliberate moral judgement that introduces a narrative sophistication.

Although no single attribute definitively identifies this friar as Dominican, the fresco's exceptional physiognomic precision elevates it beyond simple moralizing into a sophisticated form in visual theology. As shown in previous examples, in the fourteenth century, given the decline of moral behaviour within the mendicant orders, depicting friars in painting and literary works in ridiculous and derogatory ways was common practice.59 Thus, even parishioners unfamiliar with the specific conflicts between the Collegiata and the Dominican Order could find the inclusion of a friar among the sinners entirely appropriate.

The image carefully juxtaposes an innate constitutional trait—dark hair, indicating a naturally balanced temperament—from acquired features, including extreme corpulence, pale and heavy features, stuporous expression and nakedness, which reflect vice, intemperance and loss of reason. The tonsure signals religious profession and intellectual formation, yet these natural and institutional advantages contrast with the friar's wilful moral failings, rendering his damnation fully justified. With the choice to depict the friar with such careful physiognomic coding, Taddeo likely appropriates the intellectual tools of Dominican physiognomy and humoral theory, designed to instruct virtue, to critique the order itself.

6 Conclusion

Italian Last Judgement scenes often carried political and religious messages. Specifically, Giotto's Inferno in the Arena stands out as a precedent of mendicant friars integrated into hell. In this context, Taddeo di Bartolo employed an astute and original strategy, distinguishing his work from others of the period by emphasizing the obesity of the sinners in Hell—thereby suggesting a pointed and intentional visual critique. The argument of this paper—that there is a visual reference to the Dominicans of San Gimignano in the depiction of Taddeo's Hell—is grounded on several historical facts and contextual events from the period in question. This analysis argues that the prominent depiction of a single friar in the gluttons' compartment is significant. This figure is visually aligned with Dante's association of Dominicans with gluttony, as expressed in the Divina Commedia. It is also relevant that both Dante and his work were known in San Gimignano at the time. In addition, most notably, during the same period, the Dominicans were actively seeking control over San Lorenzo in Ponte—an event that situates the fresco within a broader context of ecclesiastical rivalry. Taken together, these contextual elements support reading that the fresco may serve as a subtle political or religious critique of the Dominicans.

In my opinion, Taddeo left out an unequivocal sign that he meant that friar is Dominican because he did not want to explicitly antagonize the religious order which was providing his livelihood. I think that this strategy was adopted to reveal the underlying message only to those who were familiar with specific local San Gimignano issues.

By situating the fresco within contemporary religious politics and evolving conceptions of corpulence as a moral failing, this study provides a new insight into the intersection of artistic representation, theological discourse and literary tradition in early fifteen century Italy.

1 Introduction

Upon ascending an unassuming high staircase on the west side of Piazza del Duomo in San Gimignano, visitors are in awe as they enter the church of the Collegiata di Santa Maria Assunta. The astounding walls, adorned with medieval frescoes, leave a profound impression. These largely intact frescoes were painted over a period of seventy years in the fourteenth century and the beginning of the fifteenth century. The church was known to the town's medieval inhabitants simply as the Pieve and is now referred to as the Collegiata di San Gimignano.

The south aisle features Bartolo di Fredi's Old Testament Cycle, and the north aisle showcases scenes from the New Testament. The controfacciata and adjacent walls of the nave display the Last Judgement, masterfully painted by Taddeo di Bartolo (c. 1362–1422).1 In particular, the fresco on the south wall of the central nave, positioned above the first arch of the nave arcade, vividly portrays Hell, where Taddeo explicated abstract vices by depicting specific transgressions and their corresponding punishments.2

The fresco depicts numerous sinners undergoing demonic torment, a conventional iconographic element in late medieval representations of Hell. What distinguishes this work from other Italian examples of the period is the deliberate emphasis on the corpulence of the damned. In contrast to earlier and later representations, Taddeo depicts the sinners with conspicuously obese bodies featuring distended abdomens, rounded haunches and heavily built limbs. While earlier scholarship has acknowledged the physical corpulence of these sinners, it has not explored the historical and literary contexts that might account for this portrayal.3

This paper explores Taddeo di Bartolo's portrayal of obesity in his Hell fresco, arguing that the work functioned not solely as a religious message but also as a political statement. Taddeo created this work during a conflict between the canons of the Collegiata and the Dominican Order over control of the city's religious institutions. Consequently, in response to the Dominicans' actions against their church, the patrons of the Collegiata sought a public denouncement. My thesis proposes the fresco as a visual manifestation of the power struggle and rivalry between the Collegiata and the Dominican Order.

I begin by examining the historical context through a map of San Gimignano to trace the Dominican convent's relocation within the city. This relocation sheds light on the underlying reason behind the friction: the Dominicans' unwavering determination to take over the church of San Lorenzo in Ponte, which was affiliated with the Collegiata. I present evidence demonstrating that patterns of misbehaviour and indiscipline were common among Dominican Orders in this period. Interestingly, Dante's La Divina Commedia echoes and reinforces this narrative by admonishing the moral decline of the Dominicans. Dante, who visited San Gimignano, uses their excessive indulgence in food as a symbolic representation of moral failing. The visual motif of corpulence as moral decline was part of an ongoing evolution of symbolic representations in fourteenth-century art, as exemplified by Dante's contemporary Giotto in the Scrovegni Chapel and in a miniature from the Cocharelli Codex.

In the second part of this study, my focus shifts to Taddeo's depiction of sinners in Hell and other related visual imagery. This analysis first establishes the distinctive representation of corpulent sinners in Taddeo's fresco. I then demonstrate that monumental visual art in this period frequently conveyed political messages and examine through physiognomic science how bodily and facial features served as visual markers of moral failing. Together, this evidence supports the conclusion that Taddeo's fresco functions as a political denunciation of the mendicant orders' attempt to seize control of an institution under the Collegiata's jurisdiction.

2 San Gimignano, Dante Alighieri and the Dominicans

San Gimignano, a picturesque medieval hill town in the Val d'Elsa, province of Siena, is renowned for its iconic defensive towers.4 Positioned on the historic Via Francigena, which connected Western Europe to Rome along the route between Lucca and Siena, the town held significant strategic importance in the region.5 During its golden age, at the turn of the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, San Gimignano enjoyed modest military and political prominence.6 This prominence was evident in May 1300, when Dante Alighieri was sent to the town as an ambassador of the Guelph League in Tuscany. His mission was to persuade the local officials to provide added troops in support of Pope Boniface VIII's campaign in the region.7 Dante was received by the Podestà Mino di Simone Tolomei—brother of the poet Meo, with whom Dante had exchanged sonnets.8 Given Dante's involvement in political affairs and beyond, it is reasonable to assume that he was both a familiar and esteemed figure in San Gimignano.

In the thirteenth century, the Collegiata served as the ‘Mother Church’ for several rural parishes affiliated with it.9 Among these was the Church of San Lorenzo in Ponte, situated about 250 m from the Collegiata on Via del Castello. The Dominicans, who settled in the Valdelsa in 1318, were granted the rights to build a convent on Montestaffoli, a hill located behind the Collegiata, challenging its authority.10

The Dominicans had only a brief presence at Montestaffoli. Soon after, the devastating Black Death of 1348 struck leading to a sharp population decline and political instability in San Gimignano. These crises forced the town to submit to Florence and become part of its Distretto in 1353.11

Shortly after the Florentines gained control over the land of San Gimignano, they opted to replace the strategically located Dominican institution with a fortress called Rocca di Montestaffoli. This decision was made to strengthen the Florentine's hold over the most powerful residents of the area.12 In return, the Dominicans were granted monetary compensation and a new location for their convent at the end of Via del Castello. The Dominican Order assumed control of this part of the city and reorganized its layout: they acquired the Church of S. Stefano in Canova and closed two streets, one of which ran through one of their gardens, installing gates at either end to be opened only in times of war.13

They also obtained nearby buildings from inherited estates and started construction on the sumptuous Church of the Santissima Annunziata, thus establishing a new religious presence just meters away from San Lorenzo in Ponte, which was affiliated with the Collegiata.14 Their goal was to strengthen their influence and create new lay congregations to support their growing wealth.

The map below displays the first placement of the first Dominican convent in Parco della Rocca on the left side. This location is uphill from the Collegiata di Santa Maria Assunta. On the right, we see the new location of the convent, which is situated right next to the church of San Lorenzo in Ponte (Figure 1).

The course of these events resulted in tensions between the clerics of the Collegiata and the Dominican Order in its new location. The Dominican friars sought to strengthen their spiritual jurisdiction, at the expense of the Collegiata, by requesting the Holy See to assume control of the nearby church of San Lorenzo in Ponte, which was in close proximity to their new convent. This request led to a decades-long dispute over the church's control. In the end, despite opposition from the city leaders, Pope Eugenius IV granted the Dominican's request in 1435.15

The photograph below illustrates the proximity of the Dominican convent to the church of San Lorenzo in Ponte, marked with a red circle, while a blue circle highlights a Dominican inherited estate (Figure 2 left).

Taddeo di Bartolo's Inferno was painted in the Collegiata in the second decade of the fifteenth century,16 in the timeframe between 1353 when the Dominicans moved to the new location and the Pope's injunction—in favour of the Dominincans—in 1435.

By the fourteenth century the Dominican Order was firmly established as a major institutional force within the Roman Catholic Church, but it was also facing challenges and scrutiny for corruption and moral failures.17 In his examination of primary sources regarding early Dominican discipline, Guy Geltner reports over 100 incidents of violence, implicating hundreds of friars, during the period 1250–1400.18

Contemporary figures Giovanni Villani (c. 1280–1348) and Giovanni Dominici (1356–1419) criticized the Dominicans for corruption and excess—Villani for their abuse of authority and greed, and Dominici for their lavish building projects, social luxury, neglect of communal life and misuse of funds—calling for a return to the order's original ideals of simplicity and apostolic poverty.19

Pecori records a case from San Gimignano when in 1424, a ten-year-old child from a family of parishioners of the Collegiata was taken to a Dominican convent in Siena. The incident provoked public complaints about the friars' disorderly conduct, poor governance and disregard for communal authority, which were brought before the public council in San Gimignano.20 ‘Rescue missions’ under similar circumstances in Florence and Bologna are documented in Geltner's book.21 Although I have not found additional specific references to problems with the Dominican Order in San Gimignano, there is also no indication that the town's experience differed from that of other communities in the region.

Moreover, Dante expressed his concern about the declining moral standards of the Dominican Order and made harsh comments on the challenges facing the Dominicans in Paradiso, Canto XI:

Ma 'l suo pecuglio di nova vivanda

è fatto ghiotto, sì ch'esser non puote

che per diversi salti non si spanda;

e quanto le sue pecore remote

e vagabunde più da esso vanno,

più tornano a l'ovil di latte vòte.

Ben son di quelle che temono 'l danno

e stringonsi al pastor; ma son sì poche,

che le cappe fornisce poco panno22

Dante features the Dominican friars as ghiotti, gluttonous, which symbolizes materialism and deviation from the morals of the church. He uses a flock wandering away from their shepherd, St Dominic, as a metaphor.23 The excessive indulgence in food and drink by clergymen and religious figures was a recurring theme in that period. For example, Dominican Remigio dei Girolami (c. 1235–1310) in De Bono Pacis insinuated similar behaviour. In a sermon before friars in Florence he listed the accusations of laymen toward the clergy as ‘fornicators, gluttons, rapacious, lazy’.24

There is evidence that Dante's Divina Commedia reached San Gimignano very early, likely during Dante's lifetime. As early as 1317, a notary from San Gimignano copied verses from the third canto of Inferno onto the cover of a register of criminal acts, now preserved in the State Archives of Bologna.25 Also a small fragment of Dante's Paradiso (Par. xiv 100–xxiv 60), dating from the third or last quarter of the fourteenth century and once belonging to the Useppi family of San Gimignano, was long considered lost until it was anonymously returned in 2021.26 This strongly suggests that some individuals in San Gimignano were already familiar with Dante's views on the Dominicans. This early awareness of Dante's writings could have contributed to the spread of sentiment about the Dominicans' controversial actions.

Taken together, the discussion in this section suggests that the Convent of San Domenico in San Gimignano was competing with the Collegiata. The Dominicans' presumptuous behaviour in the town was consistent with the moral crisis the order had faced since the late thirteenth century. This conduct was even admonished by Dante, whose Commedia was known in San Gimignano as early as 1317; he used the metaphor of gluttony to express his disapproval of the friars' behaviour.

In the following sections the focus shifts to Taddeo's fresco, through a comparative analysis with other artworks of a similar theme considering the evolution of corpulence as a moral signifier.

3 Corpulence as a Moral Signifier

Between the 13th and early 15th centuries, Italian visual culture developed a sophisticated vocabulary for depicting corpulence that integrated theological categories of sin, humoral medical theory and physiognomic science. Dominican theologian, Thomas Aquinas (c. 1265–1274), systematized the doctrine of gluttony27 and Albertus Magnus (c. 1200–1280) legitimated physiognomic theory within Christian natural philosophy in De Animalibus, one of the earliest medieval commentaries on ancient Greek and Arabic physiognomic ideas.28 Dominican writer Giovanni da San Gimignano (c. 1260—after 1333) argued, in the early thirteenth century, that great fatness not only ‘engenders the worst maladies’ but ‘impedes the activity of the soul’ and is thus an ‘obstacle to sense and intelligence’.29

The conclusive connection between excessive eating, gluttony and fatness emerged in the late thirteenth and early fourteenth centuries. This shift occurred within a complex landscape where multiple meanings of corpulence coexisted: in political contexts, the ‘big person’ signified vigour and power; in contexts of scarcity, corpulence marked privileged access to food; from a medical perspective, a large physique could be explained by an individual's humoral complexion or the physical demands of their profession. The mendicant orders introduced a transformative framework that revalued bodily size. Emphasizing fasting, poverty and asceticism, mendicant preachers in urban centres established thinness as a marker of spiritual authority, creating a visual rhetoric of bodily contrast between disciplined sanctity and worldly excess.30

This new moral framework demanded systematic visual representations for penitential practices. Artists responded by developing physiognomically precise depictions of sin that distinguished culpable gluttony from innocent constitutional fullness through specific markers: distended bellies, particular facial features, posture and colouring. These visual vocabularies enabled corpulence to function as a legible moral signifier within the urban penitential culture shaped by mendicant preaching. I will provide physiognomic analysis of illustrations later in this paper.

A striking example of a painting which exemplifies very clearly the concept of corpulence as a moral signifier is found in Cocharelli Codex, in Treatise on the Vices (late 14th C), BL Add MS 27695, f.14r (Figure 3). This illuminated miniature depicts the vice of gluttony through a two-tiered composition showing a tavern scene above and a wine cellar below. The upper scene features several drinkers seated at a yellow table. Heavy physique dominates the composition. The most striking feature is the pronounced corpulence of the central figures, particularly the man in a grey gown who is distinctly rounded. The figure in a blue gown beside him similarly displays a substantial, rounded body. Their flushed, reddened complexions signal drunkenness. Both hold large glasses or goblets raised to their lips, drinking in excess, their postures lacking composure. In contrast, the slimmer figure in a pink gown, on the left, sits upright and composed, his skin light and smooth, holding a small glass and drinking in moderation, providing both physical and behavioural contrast to the corpulent gluttons. Below, a cellarer with a notably rounded belly works among large wine barrels, including a prominently displayed striped barrel that emphasizes the abundant supply fuelling the excess above. The deliberate rendering of corpulence functions as a visual moral code—the swollen bodies of the drinkers and the cellarer serve as physical manifestations of their vice, making gluttony legible through flesh. This aligns with medieval physiognomic traditions where bodily form revealed inner character, the corpulent body becoming a visible sign of sin, excess and lack of temperance.

Giotto's Wedding at Cana (1304–6) in the Scrovegni Chapel offers one of the earliest European examples of corpulence as a negative moral signifier, consistent with medieval physiognomic traditions like the Secretum Secretorum and early precursor of Albertus' work.31 The master of the feast—the fat wine steward—his swollen face and reddish, upturned nose correspond to physiognomic aphorisms for a deceptive character.32 This reading is reinforced by the presence of the allegorical figure of Stultitia (Folly) in the same chapel, who is depicted with a bloated abdomen.33 The steward's substantial build contrasts with the leaner figures of Christ and the disciples, positioning him within the worldly realm of appetite and sensual pleasure as opposed to the spiritual realm of Christ's miracle. This early work demonstrates how Giotto utilized physiognomic conventions from recently translated texts to encode moral judgement through specific bodily markers (Figure 4).

When comparing the facial features of the steward and his helper to the noses of the other figures, it is important to notice that the steward has a swollen face and a reddish nose which is slanted upwards, while Christ's disciple's slender faces have straight noses, a contrast which is a legible sign of spiritual failing (Figure 5).

4 Taddeo di Bartolo and Sinners in Hell

Little information is available about the life and family of Taddeo di Bartolo (c.1362), before he is mentioned in certain documents dating back to 1382 and 1386.34 Around 1390, shortly after completing his apprenticeship, the painter began travelling for commissions, a pattern that would persist for the next decade.35 Subsequently, in the early decades of the fifteenth century, Taddeo returned to Siena and rose to become the most esteemed painter in the city. His reputation also extended to neighbouring towns, where he continued to receive prestigious commissions. Toward the end of his career, around 1420, Taddeo entered a partnership with the younger Gregorio di Cecco. However, by 1422, Taddeo had passed away.36

In the upcoming sections, I delve into Taddeo's portrayal of Hell painted in the second decade of the fifteenth century (Figure 6).37

The fresco is positioned above the first arch of the nave arcade, allowing parishioners to easily note the exaggerated corpulence of the sinners compared to the slender figures of Christ and his companions on the two adjacent walls, completing the scenes of the Last Judgement. Additionally, observers could glimpse through the arch and observe the more proportionate images of Adam and Eve—the primal sinners—depicted in the Old Testament cycle by Bartolo di Fredi, which is aligned just behind this fresco.38 These striking visual contrasts seem intentionally crafted to help parishioners decode the moral lessons linking different areas of the church via the frescoes.39

Taddeo's depiction of Hell is structured into three horizontal registers. The top one, in the lunette above the arch, depicts two zones of Hell, flanking Lucifer: Pride (superbia) on the right of Lucifer and Envy (invidia) on his left. The middle register is divided into three compartments, separated by twisted tree trunks and roots and labelled, from left to right, Gluttony (gula), Avarice (avaritia) and Lust (luxuria). The lower register around the arch is divided into two compartments also separated by a tree trunk. Though the labels on the two lowest compartments are now illegible, a comparison with the Hell scene in the Pisan Camposanto indicates that Sloth (acedia) is on the left, and Wrath (ira) is on the right.40

Among the sinners in the upper lunette, some are clearly labelled historical and biblical figures, such as Cain, Nero and Herod, while most remain unnamed (Figure 6).41

In the avarice compartment (Figure 7, left), a usurer (Usuraio) lies on his back with a dramatically bloated, distended abdomen while a demon crouches on his chest and expels golden coins into his mouth.42 His pronounced corpulence contrasts sharply with the skeletal figure hanging above him, whose emaciated form suggests he consumed his own substance for worldly goods. The demon handling this skeletal figure displays a broad, upturned nose, a physiognomic feature associated with base and bestial characteristics. In the lust compartment (Figure 7 right), a standing figure displays similar corporeal excess, with a notably rounded, protruding belly as demons torment him. Below lies a prone figure identified as a sodomite (Sotomutto). The deliberate depiction of bloated bodies in both compartments creates a visual correlation between different forms of excessive appetite—material greed and carnal lust—manifested through corpulent physiques. Throughout both scenes, flames and demonic torments intensify the punishments.

According to Mann, churchgoers would have readily recognized the behaviours of Taddeo di Bartolo's sinners, whose punishments are depicted with striking immediacy and tangible detail. He argues that parishioners could easily associate these familiar figures, shown under Lucifer's dominion, with the reality of eternal damnation.43 Viewers, in turn, might have imaginatively connected the anonymous sinners to contemporary individuals, potentially identifying specific wrongdoers. Taddeo, I suggest, employs what Mary Carruthers terms ‘associative chains’—a mnemonic strategy that guides viewers from visual stimulus to moral recollection.44 Building on this technique, I contend that Taddeo adapts the mechanism of associative memory to insinuate specific moral failings and, perhaps, to allude to particular offenders—notably the Dominicans. This method of conveying layered messages was not new in the Collegiata. Mann argues that the New Testament cycle by Bartolo di Fredi, commissioned by the commune's most powerful guild, similarly responded to the increasing competition with mendicant orders in the town.45

The following analysis presents evidence supporting the claim that Taddeo's Hell fresco features notably fleshier sinners than those typically found in fourteenth-century Italian painting. Furthermore, I will investigate how the established practice of integrating political commentary into religious frescoes likely shaped Taddeo's work. To support my thesis, I examine depictions of sinners in Hell: The Baptistery of San Giovanni, Florence (Coppo di Marcovaldo, end of the thirteenth century), Scrovegni Chapel, Padua (Giotto di Bondone, beginning of the fourteenth century), Camposanto, Pisa (Buonamico Buffalmaco, fourth decade of the fourteenth century46).47 These examples were chosen as they preceded Taddeo's fresco. More significantly, according to Solberg, Taddeo travelled to Pisa, Padua and Florence around 1390, where he likely viewed these portrayals of Hell, with the Pisan frescoes serving as his primary inspiration for Hell in the Collegiata.48 Thus, Taddeo was familiar with the bodily proportions of the damned in his artistic models. Yet he deliberately chose to paint considerably more corpulent sinners.

The mosaics in the octagonal cupola of the Baptistery of San Giovanni predate most Florentine art and were among the most prominent works of their time. Both Giotto and Dante were familiar with them in their early years. In the depiction of Hell, sinners appear emaciated, their bodies reduced to skeletal forms. Notably, some unsympathetic political figures of the time are depicted in this mosaic. For example, Brendan Cassidy writes that Pope Nicholas V has been identified in the Inferno in the Florentine Baptistery (Figure 8).49

Significantly, the sinners depicted in Giotto's fresco in the Scrovegni Chapel are not as emaciated as those found in the Florence Baptistery, yet even the more corpulent figures are not portrayed with the extreme corpulence seen in Taddeo di Bartolo's vision of Hell. The chapel holds a powerful political message, with Scrovegni himself portrayed in the foreground of the painting. Dante's own condemnation of Reginaldo's misdeeds is reinforced by the extraordinary emphasis on the punishment of usury in the lower area of Giotto's fresco.50 Notably, the painter also included tonsured clerics among the damned souls in his portrayal of Hell. Cassidy notes that Augustinian friars, whose church stood nearby, resented the chapel for drawing parishioners and for the papal indulgences attached to it. The hostility persisted for years, and the inclusion of clerics in Hell—some recognizable by their habits—may reflect these tensions.51 In summary, Giotto's fresco employs two strategies that Taddeo would later adopt: it uses Dante to corroborate condemnation of an unpopular figure, and it subtly depicts Augustinian friars in Hell reflecting power rivalries between the mendicant order and the chapel (Figure 9).

The cycle of frescoes, on the walls of the monumental Camposanto Cemetery of Pisa, were Taddeo's most likely model for his work. Baschet points out that what set the Pisan Hell apart from earlier iconographic traditions of Hell, was its bold focus on gruesome scenes of torture, drawing intense emotional reactions from viewers.52 Also in San Gimignano the focus on corporal tortures is exposed to the extreme. Unlike Taddeo's, in Buffalmacco's composition, the sinners display a range of body types, with some displaying moderate corpulence while the majority possess normative bodily proportions (Figure 10).

5 Taddeo's Depiction of Gluttony

In Taddeo's Gluttony, artistically borrowed from the Pisan Camposanto (Figure 11 left), the sinners are gathered around a round table laden with tempting food, yet they are tormented by demons who forever prevent them from eating.53 A similar theme was also portrayed by Beato Angelico's Last Judgement a few decades after Taddeo's fresco (Figure 11 right).

However, when comparing Taddeo's gluttons to those depicted by Buffalmacco and Beato Angelico, we clearly observe that Taddeo's sinners are noticeably fatter (Figure 12).

In the forefront of the gluttons' table, a contorted man struggles to shield himself from a serpent's bite while adopting a pose that exposes his lower body and suggests profound humiliation. Next to him, Taddeo distinctly emphasizes the presence of a tonsured friar. This conspicuous friar, excessively obese, finds himself coerced by a beaked demon, who forcefully twists his arm. Mann points out that the demon next to him employs a familiar gesture of silent insult, intensifying the friar's ridicule and disdain (see red circle).54 The friar's physique is characterized by a robust frame and a pronounced, rounded abdomen, displaying distinct folds of fat along the sides. His neck appears thick and fleshy, merging into his rounded shoulders. His posterior is engulfed in flames. His facial features include full, fleshy lips, a broad face and a double chin. He has a flattened, upturned nose, similar to that of the demon handling the skeletal figure described earlier. McRae's study on Albertus Magnus identifies several physiognomic traits that directly apply to Taddeo's glutton friar. A protruding belly signals wickedness, malice and deceit.55 Thick, turned-back lips indicate stupidity and voracity.56 Furthermore, a dense redness in complexion is said to reveal a deceitful nature and large, protruding ear lobes denote dullness and imprudence.57 Red hair that fades to whiteness indicates lack of understanding and an evil way of life—a colour shared by all the sinners except the friar. Conversely, black hair is recognized as one of the physiognomic characteristics of the perfect companion for kings.58 As black hair is absent from all other sinners in the fresco, this distinction may signify a deliberate moral judgement that introduces a narrative sophistication.

Although no single attribute definitively identifies this friar as Dominican, the fresco's exceptional physiognomic precision elevates it beyond simple moralizing into a sophisticated form in visual theology. As shown in previous examples, in the fourteenth century, given the decline of moral behaviour within the mendicant orders, depicting friars in painting and literary works in ridiculous and derogatory ways was common practice.59 Thus, even parishioners unfamiliar with the specific conflicts between the Collegiata and the Dominican Order could find the inclusion of a friar among the sinners entirely appropriate.

The image carefully juxtaposes an innate constitutional trait—dark hair, indicating a naturally balanced temperament—from acquired features, including extreme corpulence, pale and heavy features, stuporous expression and nakedness, which reflect vice, intemperance and loss of reason. The tonsure signals religious profession and intellectual formation, yet these natural and institutional advantages contrast with the friar's wilful moral failings, rendering his damnation fully justified. With the choice to depict the friar with such careful physiognomic coding, Taddeo likely appropriates the intellectual tools of Dominican physiognomy and humoral theory, designed to instruct virtue, to critique the order itself.

6 Conclusion

Italian Last Judgement scenes often carried political and religious messages. Specifically, Giotto's Inferno in the Arena stands out as a precedent of mendicant friars integrated into hell. In this context, Taddeo di Bartolo employed an astute and original strategy, distinguishing his work from others of the period by emphasizing the obesity of the sinners in Hell—thereby suggesting a pointed and intentional visual critique. The argument of this paper—that there is a visual reference to the Dominicans of San Gimignano in the depiction of Taddeo's Hell—is grounded on several historical facts and contextual events from the period in question. This analysis argues that the prominent depiction of a single friar in the gluttons' compartment is significant. This figure is visually aligned with Dante's association of Dominicans with gluttony, as expressed in the Divina Commedia. It is also relevant that both Dante and his work were known in San Gimignano at the time. In addition, most notably, during the same period, the Dominicans were actively seeking control over San Lorenzo in Ponte—an event that situates the fresco within a broader context of ecclesiastical rivalry. Taken together, these contextual elements support reading that the fresco may serve as a subtle political or religious critique of the Dominicans.

In my opinion, Taddeo left out an unequivocal sign that he meant that friar is Dominican because he did not want to explicitly antagonize the religious order which was providing his livelihood. I think that this strategy was adopted to reveal the underlying message only to those who were familiar with specific local San Gimignano issues.

By situating the fresco within contemporary religious politics and evolving conceptions of corpulence as a moral failing, this study provides a new insight into the intersection of artistic representation, theological discourse and literary tradition in early fifteen century Italy.
